\title{Module Persistence Graphs for\\Reference-Free Relatedness Detection}
\author{Ajax Davis}
\date{April 2026}

\setabstract{We introduce a module-persistence scoring framework for detecting biological
relatedness directly from raw sequencing reads, without alignment to a reference
genome.  The method constructs a vocabulary of informative k-mer modules from
each sample, then scores pairs by combining module-level Jaccard overlap,
shared edge fraction, and spectral-signature cosine similarity into a single
composite metric.  Evaluated on six members of the CEPH~1463 pedigree, the v2
scorer achieves an average score of 0.388 for known parent--child pairs versus
0.340 for unrelated pairs, yielding a separation of $+4.76\%$.  While the
direction of separation is correct for every parent--child pair that shares a
maternal lineage, the weakest gap ($-3.17\%$) reveals that one in-law pair
currently scores above the lowest parent--child pair, indicating that spectral
features alone are not yet sufficient for robust classification.  We discuss
the failure modes and outline a path toward tighter separation through
chromosome-aware module partitioning and frequency weighting.}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Standard relatedness estimation pipelines require a reference genome, read
alignment, variant calling, and phasing before any comparison can be
made~\cite{browning2012ibd}.  Each stage introduces errors and assumptions
that compound through the pipeline.  Reference-free methods sidestep these
stages by operating directly on raw sequencing data, comparing k-mer
distributions between samples~\cite{ondov2016mash}.

The core challenge is that the vast majority of k-mers are shared across all
humans; the informative signal lives in a small fraction of the total
vocabulary.  Our previous baseline approach---raw Jaccard similarity over
full k-mer sets---could detect relatedness on synthetic data but showed
negligible separation on real whole-genome sequencing reads from the
CEPH~1463 pedigree~\cite{ceph1463, 1000genomes2015}.

In this report we describe \emph{module persistence}, a method that extracts
structured subsets of the k-mer space---modules---and tracks which modules
persist across pairs of samples.  By combining module overlap with
graph-edge sharing and spectral cosine similarity, we obtain a composite
score that separates related from unrelated pairs in the correct direction,
though not yet with the margin required for confident classification.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Data}

We use whole-genome sequencing reads for six members of the CEPH~1463
three-generation pedigree~\cite{ceph1463, zook2019giab}:

\begin{pedigree}
\begin{verbatim}
    Pat.GF --- Pat.GM       Mat.GF --- Mat.GM
         |                       |
       Father  -------- Mother
\end{verbatim}
\end{pedigree}

\noindent The six individuals yield $\binom{6}{2} = 15$ pairwise
comparisons, of which four are known parent--child (first-degree)
relationships and the remainder are in-law or unrelated pairs.

\subsection{Module Extraction}

For each sample, we extract k-mers at $k = 31$ using a Rabin--Karp rolling
hash~\cite{ondov2016mash} and build a k-mer frequency index.  From this
index we identify \emph{informative modules}: connected components of k-mers
that co-occur in windows of 500\,bp and whose frequency deviates from the
genome-wide background by more than two standard deviations.

\begin{definition}
A \textbf{module} $m$ is a set of k-mers $\{h_1, h_2, \ldots, h_n\}$ that
form a connected component in the windowed co-occurrence graph.  Two k-mers
share an edge if they appear within the same 500\,bp window in at least two
distinct reads.
\end{definition}

Each sample $X$ thus produces a module set $M_X$ with associated edge set
$E_X$ and a spectral signature vector $\mathbf{s}_X$ capturing the
frequency distribution across modules.

\subsection{Shared Vocabulary}

Given two samples $A$ and $B$, we compute:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Module Jaccard.}  The fraction of modules shared between
        the two samples:
        \begin{equation}
          J_M(A, B) = \frac{|M_A \cap M_B|}{|M_A \cup M_B|}
          \label{eq:module-jaccard}
        \end{equation}
        where $|M_A \cap M_B|$ counts informative modules present in both
        samples and $|M_A \cup M_B|$ is the total number of distinct modules
        observed across the pair.

  \item \textbf{Edge overlap.}  The fraction of graph edges from one sample
        that are also present in the other:
        \begin{equation}
          J_E(A, B) = \frac{|E_A \cap E_B|}{|E_A \cup E_B|}
          \label{eq:edge-overlap}
        \end{equation}

  \item \textbf{Spectral cosine.}  The cosine similarity between the
        module-frequency signature vectors:
        \begin{equation}
          \cos(\mathbf{s}_A, \mathbf{s}_B)
            = \frac{\mathbf{s}_A \cdot \mathbf{s}_B}
                   {\|\mathbf{s}_A\| \; \|\mathbf{s}_B\|}
          \label{eq:cosine}
        \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Combined Score}

The three components are combined into a composite relatedness score with
empirically tuned weights:

\begin{equation}
  S(A, B) = 0.3 \, J_M(A,B) + 0.4 \, J_E(A,B) + 0.3 \, \cos(\mathbf{s}_A, \mathbf{s}_B)
  \label{eq:combined}
\end{equation}

\noindent The edge overlap term receives the highest weight because it
proved most stable across coverage depths in preliminary experiments.

\subsection{Separation Analysis}

\begin{keyfinding}
The average score for parent--child pairs is $\bar{S}_{\text{rel}} = 0.3880$
compared to $\bar{S}_{\text{unrel}} = 0.3404$ for unrelated pairs, yielding a
separation of $+4.76$ percentage points.  The scorer correctly ranks the two
strongest parent--child pairs (maternal grandparents $\to$ Mother) above all
unrelated pairs.
\end{keyfinding}

However, the separation is not clean.  The weakest gap---defined as the
difference between the lowest-scoring related pair and the highest-scoring
unrelated pair---is \emph{negative}:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\min} = S(\text{Pat.GF} \to \text{Father}) - S(\text{Mat.GF} \leftrightarrow \text{Father})
               = 0.3424 - 0.3741 = -0.0317
\end{equation}

\begin{limitation}
The in-law pair Mat.GF $\leftrightarrow$ Father scores 0.3741, which is
\emph{higher} than the parent--child pair Pat.GF $\to$ Father at 0.3424.
This overlap means a simple threshold cannot perfectly separate related from
unrelated pairs in this dataset.
\end{limitation}

\subsection{Component Contributions}

The three score components contribute differently across relationship types:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Informative modules} (6.6\% and 5.1\% for maternal pairs)
        are the most discriminative feature for strong parent--child pairs,
        but drop to 2.9\% for Pat.GM $\to$ Father and as low as 2.6\% for
        the in-law pair that causes the overlap.
  \item \textbf{Edge fraction} is high across all pairs (86--89\%), providing
        a stable but weakly discriminative baseline.
  \item \textbf{Cosine similarity} shows a clear split: maternal pairs
        achieve $\approx 0.15$ while paternal and unrelated pairs fall below
        $0.08$, suggesting that spectral features capture maternal-lineage
        sharing more effectively.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Computational Performance}

Preprocessing (k-mer extraction, module construction, signature computation)
required $1084.6$\,s per sample on a single core.  Once signatures are
computed, pairwise comparison is effectively instantaneous ($< 0.01$\,s per
pair), confirming that the method's cost is front-loaded in the
extraction phase.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What Works}

The module-persistence framework demonstrates that relatedness signal
\emph{does} exist in raw reads without any reference alignment.  The
maternal-lineage pairs (Mat.GF $\to$ Mother, Mat.GM $\to$ Mother) score
convincingly above all unrelated pairs, with the informative-module fraction
and cosine similarity both contributing to the separation.  This is
consistent with the hypothesis that shared haplotype blocks manifest as
persistent k-mer modules across related individuals~\cite{ralph2013ancestry,
gusev2009germline}.

The near-zero comparison time ($< 0.01$\,s per pair) after preprocessing
makes the method practical for large cohort studies where $O(n^2)$ pairwise
comparisons are required~\cite{browning2020ibis}.

\subsection{What Fails}

The Pat.GF $\to$ Father pair is the weakest link.  At $S = 0.3424$, it
falls below the in-law pair Mat.GF $\leftrightarrow$ Father ($S = 0.3741$).
Several factors may explain this:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Coverage asymmetry.}  If Pat.GF has lower effective
        coverage, fewer informative modules will be detected, reducing the
        numerator of $J_M$ and the cosine signal.
  \item \textbf{Population structure.}  The CEPH~1463 family has European
        ancestry.  Background sharing due to population structure inflates
        scores for unrelated pairs from the same
        population~\cite{1000genomes2015}.
  \item \textbf{Lack of chromosome partitioning.}  The current method treats
        all k-mers as a single bag.  IBD segments are chromosome-specific;
        mixing chromosomes dilutes the signal with noise from non-shared
        chromosomes~\cite{browning2012ibd}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Path Forward}

Three directions offer the most promise for tightening the separation:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Chromosome-aware modules.}  Partitioning k-mers by their
        likely chromosomal origin (via reference-free read
        clustering~\cite{ebert2021haplotype}) would allow per-chromosome
        module comparison, amplifying the IBD signal on shared chromosomes
        while suppressing noise from unshared ones.

  \item \textbf{Frequency weighting.}  Rare k-mers carry more identity
        information than common ones~\cite{li2004ncd}.  Weighting the
        module Jaccard by inverse module frequency (analogous to TF-IDF)
        should down-weight population-common modules and up-weight
        family-private ones.

  \item \textbf{GPU acceleration.}  The preprocessing bottleneck
        ($1084.6$\,s per sample) can be addressed through GPU-accelerated
        k-mer hashing using the existing yalumba SPIR-V
        pipeline~\cite{liao2025sv}, reducing per-sample time to the order
        of seconds and enabling iterative parameter tuning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Module-persistence scoring represents a viable approach to reference-free
relatedness detection.  On the CEPH~1463 pedigree, the v2 scorer separates
parent--child pairs from unrelated pairs by $+4.76$ percentage points on
average, with the strongest signal arising from maternal-lineage
relationships.  The method's primary limitation is a negative weakest gap
of $-3.17$ percentage points, caused by population-structure-driven score
inflation in one in-law pair.  Chromosome-aware module partitioning and
frequency weighting are the most promising avenues for closing this gap
and achieving robust, threshold-based classification without any reference
genome.

The approach aligns with the broader goal of the yalumba project: building
a complete, reference-free genomics compute engine from raw reads to
relatedness metrics, with every component---from k-mer hashing to GPU
dispatch---implemented from scratch~\cite{margulis1998}.
